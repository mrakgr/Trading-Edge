# Taker Fill Simulator — Design Document

## Purpose

The current backtest of the 1h MA mean-reversion crypto system uses 1m bar-close prices as fill prices, which is functionally a "frictionless taker" assumption. This overstates achievable performance because it ignores spread cost, latency, and the fact that some signals fire in moments where insufficient liquidity exists to fill at a representative price.

The taker fill simulator estimates a realistic taker fill price for each signal using only trade-tape data (no order book required). Its outputs feed back into the backtest to produce a more honest estimate of strategy edge.

A secondary goal is robustness analysis: the simulator's parameters are not perfectly knowable in advance, so the design exposes them as sweepable inputs and reports edge across a parameter grid rather than at a single point.

## Inputs

For each signal generated by the strategy:

- **Signal timestamp** `t_signal` (nanosecond precision)
- **Side** (buy or sell)
- **Instrument**

Plus, from the trade tape:

- **Trades** within `[t_signal, t_signal + W_max]` where `W_max` is the hard time cap (default 3s)
- Each trade has timestamp, price, size, and aggressor side (buyer-initiated or seller-initiated)

## Output

For each signal, one of:

- **Filled**: a simulated fill price `p_fill`, plus diagnostic metadata (number of trades used, window duration, etc.)
- **Scratched**: signal is excluded from the strategy entirely (no entry, no PnL contribution)

## Algorithm

### Step 1: Filter to relevant trades

Keep only trades within the time window `[t_signal + t_skip, t_signal + W_max]`.

- `t_skip = 200ms` (default): discards trades during the latency/routing window. These trades couldn't have been your fill in reality because your order wouldn't have arrived yet.
- `W_max = 3s` (default): hard upper bound on how long to wait. Beyond this, we are no longer modeling a fill — we are averaging the trade itself.

Then filter to **trades on the side you are taking from**:

- For a buy entry: keep only buyer-aggressive trades (trades that lifted the offer)
- For a sell entry: keep only seller-aggressive trades (trades that hit the bid)

This is critical. Trades on the opposite side aren't fills you could have gotten — they're the other side of the book transacting. Including them in the count and average produces a misleading picture of clearable liquidity on your side.

For Binance trade data, aggressor side is given by the `isBuyerMaker` flag:
- `isBuyerMaker = false` → buyer-aggressive (lifted offer)
- `isBuyerMaker = true` → seller-aggressive (hit bid)

For data without aggressor flags, fall back to the tick rule (price > prior mid → buyer-aggressive, etc.), but this should be a fallback, not the default.

### Step 2: Liquidity threshold check

Count the same-side trades from Step 1. Call this `n_trades`.

If `n_trades < N_min` (default 10), **scratch the signal**. The instrument was too illiquid in this window for a realistic taker fill to be meaningful.

This is the most important property of the simulator: it must be willing to say "no fill" as one of its outputs. A simulator that always produces a fill price will systematically overstate edge in illiquid regimes — which, given the strategy's characteristics, is precisely where the apparent edge is largest.

### Step 3: Compute fill price via exponentially-tilted weighted average

For the trades passing Steps 1 and 2, compute:

```
p_fill = sum(w_i * size_i * price_i) / sum(w_i * size_i)
```

where:

```
w_i = exp(-(t_i - t_signal - t_skip) / tau)
```

- `tau` is the exponential half-life parameter (default: 500ms, i.e., decay rate `ln(2) / 500ms`)
- Earlier trades within the window contribute more than later ones
- Size weighting (`size_i`) reflects that larger prints are more representative of clearable liquidity

The exponential tilt models the reality that if a fill happens at all, it happens nearer the start of the available window, not the end. Trades late in the window are increasingly "trades that happened while you'd already be in the position" rather than "trades that contributed to your fill."

### Step 4: Apply taker fee

After computing `p_fill`, apply the taker fee (default: 5bp on Binance USD-M perps, but parameterize per venue/instrument).

For a buy: effective entry price = `p_fill * (1 + fee)`
For a sell: effective entry price = `p_fill * (1 - fee)`

The same logic is applied to exits. (If the strategy holds the position for `~2 minutes` and exits at the bar close of an exit signal, the exit fill should be modeled symmetrically with the same simulator.)

## Parameters and defaults

| Parameter | Default | Sweep range for robustness analysis |
|-----------|---------|-------------------------------------|
| `t_skip` | 200ms | 100ms, 200ms, 500ms |
| `W_max` | 3s | 2s, 3s, 5s |
| `N_min` | 10 | 5, 10, 20 |
| `tau` (exp half-life) | 500ms | 200ms, 500ms, 1s, 1.5s |
| `taker_fee` | 5bp (perps) | venue-dependent |

## Reporting

Per backtest run, report:

- **Fill rate**: fraction of signals that produced a fill (i.e., not scratched). A fill rate below ~60% suggests the strategy is heavily concentrated in illiquid signals; a fill rate near 100% suggests the strategy mostly fires in liquid regimes.
- **Mean and median realized PnL per filled trade** (in bps)
- **Total PnL after scratching, accounting for the reduced trade count**
- **Distribution of `n_trades` across signals**: useful for diagnosing whether the strategy's edge is concentrated near the `N_min` threshold (which would suggest sensitivity to that parameter)
- **Edge sensitivity table**: PnL as a function of `tau` and `N_min`, holding others at defaults

## Robustness criteria

The strategy is considered to have a defensible taker edge if:

1. **Mean PnL per filled trade is positive across the entire parameter sweep** (all combinations of `tau` and `N_min` in the sweep range). If edge is positive only at the optimistic corner of the grid, the result is fragile.
2. **Fill rate does not collapse** (no parameter combination produces fill rate below ~30%). If the only way to get positive edge is to scratch most signals, the strategy is too narrow to be useful.
3. **Total PnL (after scratching) remains positive across the sweep**. Per-trade edge times trade count is what actually matters.

If only criteria (1) and (3) hold but fill rate varies wildly, that's still a tradeable result — just with a smaller effective trade count than the naive backtest suggested.

## Optional: volume-cumulative variant

A parallel implementation worth building as a sanity check. Instead of a fixed time window:

- Specify intended order size `S` (e.g., the size you'd actually trade in production)
- Accumulate same-side trades from `t_signal + t_skip` until cumulative size reaches `k * S` (e.g., `k = 3`) or `W_max` is hit
- Fill price = VWAP of trades in that accumulated window (no exponential tilt needed, since the cumulative-size cutoff is doing the work)
- Scratch if cumulative size never reaches `k * S` within `W_max`

This variant has a cleaner interpretation ("you fill against approximately 3x your size in available flow before giving up") but requires committing to a target order size. If the time-and-count and volume-cumulative variants agree directionally on which signals are profitable, that's strong evidence the result is robust to fill-modeling choices.

## Implementation notes

- F# is the natural language given the existing pipeline. The simulator should consume Parquet trade tapes (DuckDB-readable) and produce a Parquet file of per-signal fill outcomes.
- Per-instrument processing: trade tapes can be large, so process one instrument at a time and aggregate results.
- Use nanosecond timestamps throughout; the `t_skip = 200ms` granularity matters and should not be lost to second-level rounding.
- The aggressor-side filter is the most error-prone part. Test it on a small sample by hand before running at scale: the filtered trade list should look like the upticks (for buy entries) on a price chart of the relevant window.
- Performance: at sweep granularity (4 `tau` × 3 `N_min` = 12 configs) and a few thousand signals per instrument, this is well within in-memory processing. No need to optimize prematurely.

## Out of scope

- Modeling maker fills (this requires order book or live data; the maker case is being evaluated separately, likely via live testing).
- Modeling exit slippage when the strategy uses limit-order exits (the current strategy uses time/MA-based exits at bar close, which is symmetric to entry and uses the same simulator).
- Modeling funding payments (handled at the strategy level, not the fill level).
- Modeling latency variance or partial fills explicitly. The exponential tilt and `N_min` threshold are stand-ins for these effects; if the live system later reveals systematic biases, they can be added as refinements.

## Next steps after implementation

1. Run the simulator with default parameters on the existing backtest signal set.
2. Run the parameter sweep and produce the robustness report.
3. If results pass the robustness criteria, proceed to live testing (small size, single instrument) to validate that real fills land within the simulator's predicted range.
4. If results fail the robustness criteria, the apparent edge in the bar-close backtest is most likely a fill artifact, and the strategy should be either redesigned or shelved.
